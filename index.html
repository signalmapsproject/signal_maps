Section 01

Source visibility overview

The center of gravity in English-language discovery was heavily weighted toward U.S. domestic media, with only partial visibility for some international outlets. The question is not only what was absent, but where the signal appeared instead and how that shaped first impressions.

Visible

High-discovery outlets

U.S. domestic outlets and a limited set of international brands were easy to surface, shaping first impressions and baseline framing.

CNN · NBC · NPR · PBS · CBS · Washington Post

Partial

Present but unstable

Some outlets appeared inconsistently through snippets, delayed indexing, or secondary references, making them present but unstable inside the discovery layer.

Al Jazeera · BBC · Wikipedia

Absent or poorly surfaced

Asymmetric visibility

Wire services, foreign public broadcasters, and several state-media ecosystems were difficult to surface directly in ordinary search, creating asymmetric visibility rather than a neutral field.

Reuters · CGTN / Xinhua · IRNA / Press TV · RT · Haaretz · France 24

Proxy leakage

Landstuhl medical strain

The Landstuhl labor-and-delivery suspension matters because hospital operations can function as unusually strong proxy indicators when direct casualty disclosure is thin. It is consistent with elevated medical strain, but it does not by itself quantify intake or prove a hidden total.

Section 04

The method in brief

The Exoplanet Method starts with what is visible, then tests what appears absent across source ecosystems, time, and material systems. It treats absence as a lead rather than proof and distinguishes verified facts from probable inferences, open hypotheses, disputed claims, and narrative framing.

Exoplanet Method

Absence is data — but only after testing

Absences must be classified, their basis made explicit, their routing tracked, and rival explanations tested. The method is strongest as an investigative discipline, not a license to convert silence into proof or treat search failure as censorship.

Section 06

Signal Ledger

The Signal Ledger is the project’s timeline-first public record. Instead of listing open questions in the abstract, it tracks when an event happened, when it entered the record, how it moved across source ecosystems, what kind of claim it is, and what remains unresolved.

Verification structure

Claims stay graded

  • Each ledger row distinguishes verified facts, proxy indicators, disputed claims, narrative claims, and open hypotheses.
  • Confidence, routing, and resolution status stay attached instead of disappearing into summary prose.
Timeline structure

Event time vs signal time

  • The ledger separates when something allegedly happened from when it became visible in the record.
  • That makes delay, disappearance, and late attribution part of the evidence rather than a side note.
Open work

Unresolved questions stay alive

  • Rows keep rival explanations, what would resolve them, and whether a point is open, contested, or resolved.
  • The ledger functions as both watchlist and archive, linking back to the deeper case pages.

Section 07

About this project

Signal Maps is a research and analysis project about conflict visibility: what appears in the discovery layer, what gets delayed or softened, and what becomes legible only through proxy indicators or rerouted sources. It is designed to be read as a structured field of signals rather than a single verdict.

What this homepage does

Front door, not full archive

This page introduces the project, shows the source visibility overview, previews a small set of key signals, and routes readers to dedicated pages for deeper analysis.

What this project does not do

No claim is self-proving here

The project distinguishes verified facts, probable inferences, open hypotheses, disputed claims, and narrative framing. It treats omission and delay as investigable patterns rather than automatic proof of concealment.

Read the homepage for orientation. Read the cases, timeline, ledger, and method to see how the project actually handles uncertainty.

Signal Maps organizes the project in layers: homepage for orientation, cases for close reading, method for safeguards, timeline for sequence, ledger for structured tracking, and scorecard for AI comparison.
