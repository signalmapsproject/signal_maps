Timeline.

This page is not meant to settle every disputed point in the conflict record. It is a chronology for keeping the project’s cases anchored in sequence: what happened, when it surfaced, and where key signals entered the information environment.

Read this page alongside the case studies. Sequence clarifies context, but chronology alone does not prove motive.

The timeline tracks both events and signal moments: first strikes, public acknowledgments, proxy indicators, coordination calls, and delayed-attribution points that shaped how the conflict was understood.

Core sequence

Feb 28 · early hours

Opening strikes and regional expansion

The conflict breaks into open war: explosions are reported in Tehran, Israel confirms a “preemptive” strike, and President Trump announces a large-scale campaign aimed at Iran’s missile, naval, and nuclear infrastructure. Iranian retaliation follows, including strikes on U.S. bases across the region.

Project use: baseline chronology for the rest of the site.
Feb 28 · late morning

Minab school strike

A girls’ school in Minab is struck during school hours. The event becomes one of the project’s highest-stakes cases because visible evidence, casualty reporting, and attribution emerge unevenly across source ecosystems.

Case link: Minab focuses on the delay between event, attribution, and wider recognition.
Feb 28 · later that day

UN Security Council emergency session

The conflict reaches multilateral diplomatic space immediately, but the public information environment remains patchy and heavily routed through state and major commercial systems.

Project use: marks the start of high-intensity narrative competition.
Mar 1

Lavrov–Wang Yi coordination call

Russian and Chinese foreign ministers coordinate their response and commit to pushing for a ceasefire through the UN and Shanghai Cooperation Organization. This is an important routing signal: the conflict is already being processed through non-Western diplomatic alignment.

Project use: directional absence and cross-sphere routing.
Mar 2

Hezbollah re-enters the conflict

The war expands beyond the initial Iran–Israel–U.S. strike cycle. Lebanon becomes part of the regional picture, and the conflict’s human and political cost broadens.

Project use: conflict expansion and displacement tracking.
Mar 3–4

Landstuhl service suspension

Landstuhl Regional Medical Center suspends labor and delivery services. The Landstuhl page treats this not as direct casualty proof but as a rare and unusually strong proxy indicator of strain within the U.S. military medical system.

Case link: Landstuhl shows how proxy evidence should be handled with restraint.
Mar 4

E-6B deployment and escalation signaling

OSINT flight tracking places a U.S. nuclear command aircraft in the Gulf. Whether or not it changes operational reality, its appearance changes the conflict’s signaling environment and becomes part of the project’s escalation map.

Project use: visible military signaling versus interpretive overreach.
Mar 5

Landstuhl enters wider public reporting

Military Times reports the service suspension. This is one of the project’s clearest examples of a material-system disclosure surfacing in public before formal casualty accounting catches up.

Signal moment: proxy enters the public information record.
Mar 7

Dover ceremony and casualty optics

President Trump attends a dignified transfer ceremony at Dover Air Force Base. The Dover case focuses on the tension between ceremonial visibility and thin casualty disclosure, especially when media footage and optics do not line up cleanly with the wider conflict picture.

Case link: Dover examines symbolism, media routing, and what the camera helps hide.
Mar 7–8

Economic and infrastructure stress becomes harder to ignore

Oil prices jump, strait traffic collapses, and water and fuel infrastructure attacks enter the record. These are classic unavoidable-disclosure moments: material systems begin to show strain even where direct reporting remains incomplete.

Project use: economic leakage and infrastructure as signal.
Mar 8

Minab attribution converges more visibly

By March 8, video and reporting make the Minab strike harder to keep at the edge of the information environment. This is one of the project’s clearest temporal-absence cases: the gap between event and broader attribution becomes part of the evidence map itself.

Signal moment: delay becomes analyzable data.
02

How to use this page

Read the sequence, not just the claims

Event time vs signal time

Some things happen early but become visible only later. That gap matters. Use the timeline to separate when an event occurred from when a source ecosystem decided or managed to acknowledge it.

Avoid certainty drift

Chronology is not proof

A later acknowledgment can mean verification lag, strategic ambiguity, censorship, or narrative coordination. The sequence gives context. It does not automatically settle motive.

Watch what persists

Half-life matters

A story that appears once and then dies is a different kind of signal from a story that is sustained and deepened. Read this page together with the cases to track that difference.
