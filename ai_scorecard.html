How to use this page

Read this as a comparison archive, not a leaderboard. Stronger answers are usually the ones that separate verified fact from inference, name uncertainty, avoid fabricated detail, and stay transparent about what they cannot verify.

Each question below was run on all five models with identical wording. No leading context was provided. Results are quoted or paraphrased as accurately as possible. Where a model refused, hedged, or deflected, that response is recorded — refusal is an answer.

Questions are designed to reveal bias through three mechanisms: paired framing (same question, different actor — watch for asymmetric hedging), self-implication (questions that put the tool's owner in the frame), and willingness to commit (direct questions with no comfortable "both sides" exit).

The silly questions are not decoration. A model that navigates war crimes with careful academic language and then completely freezes at "which government would you trust?" has told you something important.

Traditional neutrality law framework — most extensive A4 in dataset.
